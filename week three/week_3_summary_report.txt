Week 3 Summary Report: Machine Learning Fundamentals
Program: AnalystLab Africa Machine Learning Internship 

What I Learned This Week
This week focused on transitioning from data preparation to the foundational principles of training and evaluating machine learning algorithms, primarily utilizing the Titanic and Iris datasets. 

* Supervised vs. Unsupervised Learning: Differentiated between training models on labeled ground-truth data (Classification and Regression) versus finding hidden patterns in unlabeled data (Clustering).
* Data Splitting: Implemented train_test_split via Scikit-Learn, partitioning data into 70% training and 30% testing sets to ensure models are evaluated on unseen data prior to deployment.
* The Bias-Variance Tradeoff: Explored the mechanics of underfitting (too simple, high bias) and overfitting (memorizing noise, high variance).
* Visualizing Complexity: Built a Scikit-Learn Pipeline utilizing a DecisionTreeClassifier and plotted 2D decision boundaries to visually demonstrate how altering tree depth (from a simple line at depth 1 to jagged islands at depth 20) impacts generalization.
* Advanced Model Evaluation: Trained a LogisticRegression model and moved beyond standard accuracy. Evaluated performance using Precision, Recall, and a visually rendered Confusion Matrix to better understand True/False Positives and Negatives.

Challenges Encountered
* Navigating the Sweet Spot: Finding the exact point of model complexity where the algorithm captures underlying relationships without crossing into overfitting requires careful visual and metric-driven tuning.
* Metric Interpretation: Grasping the practical difference between Precision and Recall, especially when deciding whether it is more important to minimize False Positives or False Negatives depending on the specific problem domain.
* Pipeline Coordination: Ensuring that feature engineering (like extracting passenger titles) and imputation methods are correctly cleanly passed through the model pipeline before generating predictions.

Key Insights Gained
* Accuracy is Not Enough: In imbalanced scenarios (like the Titanic survival rates), a high accuracy score can be deeply misleading. A Confusion Matrix is essential for painting the full picture of a model's predictive behavior. 
* Generalization is the Ultimate Goal: A model that scores 100% on training data is often useless in a real-world context. Testing on an isolated split is a non-negotiable step for confirming deployment readiness.
* Visualization Drives Understanding: Transforming abstract concepts like overfitting into a visual data story—such as mapping out multidimensional decision boundaries on a 2D plot—makes technical performance failures instantly recognizable and actionable.
